Proactive Assistance in M365 Mobile

Building a scalable UX framework for AI-driven nudges across Copilot, Outlook and Teams — shifting M365 mobile from a reactive information system to one that anticipates what you need next.

Role
Senior Product Designer
Platform
M365 Copilot · Outlook · Teams Mobile
Scope
UX Framework · Flows · End-to-End Ownership
Team
PM · Engineering · Data · Design
The Problem

M365 had all the signals. Users still had to do all the hunting.

The apps were full of rich data — upcoming meetings, unread emails, pending tasks, AI agent outputs. But none of it surfaced proactively. Users had to open each app, navigate to the right place, and mentally piece together what needed their attention.

On mobile especially, this felt exhausting. Short attention spans, constant context-switching, and a UI built for consumption rather than action meant that the most important things often got missed.

"The challenge wasn't a lack of intelligence in the system — it was that none of that intelligence was working on the user's behalf."

The Goal

Surface the right action at the right moment — without creating noise.

Proactive assistance had to earn its place. Push it too hard and it feels intrusive. Make it too subtle and it gets ignored. The design challenge was threading that needle — and doing it in a way that scaled across multiple apps and surfaces.

  • Help users act faster, not just stay more informed
  • Make proactive help feel timely, lightweight, and easy to dismiss
  • Create a reusable design framework other teams and agents could build on
My Approach

A framework first, not a feature first.

Rather than designing individual proactive moments in isolation, I built a minimal, repeatable framework that every proactive experience had to follow:

Trigger → Actionable Response → Feedback

This gave PMs, engineers and designers a shared language and a clear bar for what "good" looks like. It prevented one-off designs that couldn't be maintained, and ensured quality consistency across apps and agent types.

Key Design Decisions

Every choice filtered through one question: does this help the user act?

01

Action over insight

Every proactive surface had to answer "What can I do right now?" — not just "Here's something you might want to know." If there was no clear, immediate action, the moment didn't ship.

02

Mobile-first restraint

Limited screen space meant fewer options and clearer hierarchy. A maximum of one primary action and one optional secondary action per card. No nested menus, no expanded flows unless explicitly triggered.

03

Progressive disclosure

Detail was available when the user leaned in — not pushed upfront. Cards led with a single line of context and a clear action. More information appeared only on tap or expand.

04

User control at every step

Snooze, dismiss, or ignore — all zero-penalty options. Feedback loops (completed, dismissed, irrelevant) fed back into future relevance. The system had to get smarter through use, not force engagement.

What I Designed

Patterns that scaled across the M365 ecosystem.

  • Dynamic Action surfaces that adapt based on user context (meeting about to start, unread email from exec, task due today)
  • Action card anatomy: one-line context → clear primary action → optional secondary → dismiss
  • Feedback loops (dismiss, complete, snooze) to improve AI relevance over time
  • Framework applied to Daily Brief, inbox moments, meeting prep, and agent-led actions
  • Documentation and design guidance used by 3+ partner teams building on the system

What I'd Do Differently

Learnings worth keeping.

  • Test interruption frequency thresholds earlier — fine-tuning the line between helpful and noisy is hard to do retroactively
  • Bring engineering into framework reviews sooner — feasibility feedback early prevents expensive pivots late
